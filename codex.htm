GROUP PROJECT | 8 WEEKS | PRODUCT DESIGN + MOBILE APP

 

PROJECT BACKGROUND

Codex started as a class project for the “User Centered Design” class at University of Washington.

Many people face the challenges of doing many things with little time, and the frustrations of not having enough time to do what they want to do. In this project, we want to explore innovative ways to help people manage tasks, and eventually having more meaningful time for themselves.

Project outcome

 

Codex consists of 2 parts:

1. A physical paradigm for task identification and organization using hexes.

 

These hexes allow the user to physically interact with tasks. The position and grouping of the hexes help the user organize their tasks in an intuitive way. The screens provide visual clues of how much of a task is done.

 
 

2. A companion app

The companion app allows the user to take the hex-based distribution of tasks on the go. Codex also implements a powerful task-analysis AI in the app that ‘takes over’ for users in the case of menial or simple tasks.

PROCESS

 
 

Exploratory research

Part 1 - INITIAL DESIGN QUESTION

 

The design question we started with was:

“How can we help creatives integrate structure into their daily routine while retaining a fluid approach to inspiration? ”

“Creatives” here refers to those who typically work in bursts of inspiration, handling tasks that require constant dedication, longer focus, and are comprised of many subtasks. We started the project by theorizing that creatives could benefit from a novel, hybrid schedule that would add structure to their daily routine while retaining sufficient fluidity for inspirational bursts. For exploratory research, we explored the topic to refine our design question.

 
 
 

SURVEY

Next, we aimed to gather data through surveys to quantitatively determine if there were any demographic or behavioral patterns that could be correlated with productivity.

Findings:

1. 52 participants self-classified as productive and 21 as unproductive. Both groups reported challenges meeting their long-term goals.

2. Focusing on long-term goals appears to help accomplish more tasks, whether those tasks are short- or long-term.

3. Long-term planning seems to be correlated with anxiety and stress.

 
 
 
 

interview

 

We conducted interviews on 8 participants and 2 key findings were:

1. Participants felt that a significant chunk of their time was 'wasted' on interactions and activities that are MENIAL. They wanted to spend less time on those activities and instead devote their time on more useful, meaningful activities.

 
 

2. From the 'give me your craziest idea of an ideal time management system' question, we learned that they wish to have a device (AI) that can intelligently manage/categorize their activities, compile data from multiple sources, or even complete tasks in the participant’s name.

fOCUSED RESEARCH

 

We used contextual inquiry for more focused research.

Findings:

1. Participants tend to use more than one tool, and the tools frequently cross the virtual/physical space.

2. Physically “dealing with” and “completing tasks” (e.g moving around sticky notes, crossing out completed tasks) are satisfying and stress relieving.

 
 


Quantitative Assessment

 

In order to determine our persona groups, we went through a quantitative assessment on the data we gained through surveys, focusing on a high/low classification for the 4 main criteria we’ve selected (which we called the ‘OTSG’ acronym):

  • Organization level

  • Technology use

  • Stress level

  • Goals focus (Long-term vs Short-term)

 
 

persona

 
 

Design requirement


Based on the user research analysis and persona definition, we have identified several distinct user needs areas that the final solution must satisfy:

 
 

1. A system that alleviates stress by reducing the amount of trivial tasks and subtasks they have to complete in a day.

2. A system that analyzes schedule and optimizes the amount of time spent on tasks.

3. A simple and intuitive way of creating and visualizing their schedule.

 
 
 

brainstorm

To explore the various creative avenues exposed by our user research, we utilized several idea generation methods. 

First, the N-3-5 method was used to create a large amount of initial ideas in small amount of time. Each of us individually generated 3 ideas in 5 minutes. Each idea sheet is then passed to another team member. Using the existing sketches as inspirations, we each developed three new ideas in another 5 minutes. At the end of this method, we came up with 48 individual ideas in approximately 20 minutes.

 
 

After the N-3-5 session, we went through a group brainstorm session.

WIREFRAME

USABILITY TEST 1

 

For the first set of usability test, we examined the basic elements of our time management system by asking users to perform the following tasks and to answer a user feedback questionnaire.

 
 

We carried out the usability tests on 4 participants; on each of them, we tested the usability of both the physical device and the app, and got general feedback about the design and the integration of the time management system. 

 
 

We identified and summarized 20 issues during the usability tests. Among these issues, 3 of them were particularly critical:

1. Participants experienced confusion and frustration with the navigation system we implemented in our apps.

2. The hexes did not offer visual cues to help participants figure out how to use them. 

3. The terminology (hexes, groups, tasks, categories) was confusing to the users.

 
 
 

USABILITY TEST 2

For the second set of usability test, we updated our app design based on the feedback we obtained from the Usability Test 1 and built a prototype of the physical hexes hardware to test the interactions between the mobile app and the physical hexes. 

 
 
 

The hardware hexes we built were able to:

1. Change screen and colors when “grouped”.

2. Sync and change to different screens as users operate on the mobile app.

3. Allow tasks to be completed when users tap on the click box on the physical hex.

 

Findings from the 2nd usability test:

1. Hex map should be more accessible through front page. Alternating between different views (hex map, group view, task view) should be easier and more intuitive.

2. Users would like to have a way to recover “completed” task in case they “completed” it by accident.

3. Clicking on the top right corner to return to home page is not intuitive to most users.

 
 

iterationS

 

These insights above informed the decisions of our final prototypes. We updated the hexagon user interface to improve their intuitiveness and corrected the navigation and language usability issues on the companion app.

 
 

Iterations based on Usability Test 1 from last section:

For physical hexes, there are some main changes:

  1. A checkbox is added on task hexes. Users can simply tap on the checkbox on the hex (to avoid confusion - “how do I complete a task on the hex?”)

  2. Set the default color of the ungroup task hexes to grey, visually distinguishing the ungroup tasks from the group tasks

 
 

For mobile app, 1st - 2nd version iteration include changes such as:

  1. Navigation to home: from a vertical slide bar to an icon

  2. Add “Hex Map” feature

  3. Make home page less overwhelming/confusing

  4. Move the submission button from top of the page to the bottom 

  5. Split the Hub page to “Insights” and “AI”

  6. Simplify the language and clarify the terminology

 
 
 

Iterations based on Usability Test 2 from last section:

For mobile app, 2nd - 3rd version iteration include changes such as:

  1. Navigation to home: from an icon on the top right corner to a back button

  2. Insert “Hex Map” feature (in “My Hexes” section in previous version) to the Home page.

  3. Allow users to alternate between tabs more easily. For example, users can switch among “Hex Map”, “Group View”, and “Task View” by alternating tabs (or zooming in/out and click on the specific group/task) instead of going to different pages

  4. The tasks are crossed out and turning grey after users “complete” them; users can also “undo” the task

  5. More freedom/customization for sharing/creating a task
